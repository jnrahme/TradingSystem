Multi-Strategy Trading Platform Redesign

This redesign turns the current trading codebase into a reusable platform where the trading system and the strategy brain are isolated on purpose. The platform owns data, execution, risk, portfolio state, replay, promotion, and dashboards. Strategies become plugins that can be attached, paper traded, replayed, promoted, and compared without rewriting the rest of the stack.

Final Answer

Are we using the correct trading platform? No single broker is the right answer for options, penny stocks, and crypto all at once. The correct answer is a broker-agnostic core with multiple adapters. Use one internal paper broker as the primary testing venue, then route live orders through the best broker per asset class.
Is the system solid? Not yet, if strategy logic can still touch execution and file-based state directly. A solid system has hard boundaries: immutable event history, canonical portfolio ledger, broker reconciliation, risk at multiple layers, and promotion gates between research, paper, and live.
How should we trade today in order to win? Start with liquid, data-rich, mechanically testable edges. That means index and ETF options first, major crypto second, penny stocks last and paper-only until the fraud, liquidity, and halt filters prove themselves.
Best architecture choice A modular monolith with event-driven seams is the best first architecture. It keeps the system auditable and understandable now, while preserving a clean path to split out heavy domains later.
Recommended platform thesis: build the platform around an internal execution simulator, a strategy plugin SDK, a portfolio ledger, a risk engine, and a broker capability registry. Do not hard-wire the business to Alpaca, IBKR, Coinbase, or any single venue.
Operating rule for now: paper-only until a strategy proves it is worth keeping. Live trading support may exist in the architecture, but it should remain locked behind explicit promotion criteria and manual approval.

What the options market is saying

Cboe reported that total volume across its four options exchanges reached 4.6 billion contracts in 2025, with 18.4 million contracts of average daily volume, marking a sixth straight record year. Cboe also described the broader listed-options market as having topped 15.2 billion contracts in 2025, 26% above 2024.

What that means for strategy prioritization

Liquid options and major crypto venues have cleaner data, clearer fills, better automation surfaces, and stronger market structure than penny stocks. Penny and microcap names remain unusually exposed to manipulation, scarce public information, and low liquidity, which is exactly why they belong behind extra filters and later rollout gates.

The first serious strategies should therefore be liquid, rules-driven, and easy to replay and paper trade: index/ETF options, large-cap equity strategies, and major crypto pairs.

Trading Platform Decision

The right answer is not “pick one broker forever.” The right answer is “build a broker abstraction, then use the strongest venue per job.” Public broker docs show real differences in asset coverage, paper behavior, market data, and order models.

Platform What the docs show Strength Limitation Verdict
IBKR Public IBKR materials and API docs cover stocks, options, futures, forex, bonds, paper trading, and API automation. Best long-term core for broad multi-asset coverage. Operationally heavier than simpler retail APIs. Use as the main non-crypto production adapter.
Alpaca Public docs show one Trading API across equities, crypto, and options, with paper options enabled by default and multi-leg options supported. Fastest early iteration path for U.S. equities, crypto, and options. Not broad enough to become the only forever venue. Use as an early paper and dev adapter, not the sole platform.
Tradier Public docs emphasize U.S. equities and options, a sandbox, full trading API coverage, delayed sandbox market data, and support for complex multi-leg options. Excellent options-focused broker adapter candidate. U.S. equities and options only. Use as optional options specialist adapter.

Target Architecture

The winning architecture is a modular monolith with event-driven seams. That gives us one understandable codebase today, while preserving clean domain boundaries so heavy components can be split out later without rewriting every strategy.

Why this is better than a microservices-first design

Microservices would create extra latency, deployment complexity, and coordination overhead before the platform has stable contracts. Right now the winning move is to isolate the domains in code and data first, not to distribute everything on day one.

Why this is better than a single giant script or notebook stack

A giant quant monolith collapses strategy, execution, and state into one place. That is fast at first, but it makes it almost impossible to promote strategies safely, compare them fairly, or reason about broker-specific failures.

Isolation Boundaries

Domain Owns Must Not Know Reusable?
Strategy SDK Strategy manifest, context contract, state contract, intent contract. Broker APIs, database internals, deployment details. Yes. Reused by every strategy family.
Strategy Runtime Plugin loading, event delivery, state snapshots, run orchestration. Venue-specific order payloads and secret handling. Yes. Same runtime for options, penny, and crypto.
Risk Engine Limits, exposure checks, kill switches, drawdown rules, product gates. Strategy alpha logic and UI rendering. Yes. Global and strategy-level policy reuse.
Execution Engine Routing, order translation, retries, reconciliation, broker state sync. Alpha generation and feature engineering. Yes. Shared across all strategies and brokers.
Portfolio Ledger Canonical positions, fills, PnL, capital accounting. Strategy heuristics and broker authentication flow. Yes. Core source of truth for all dashboards.
Market Data Normalization, enrichment, replay snapshots, feature surfaces. Order execution and strategy state mutation. Yes. Shared by all runtimes and research tools.
Replay / Simulator Historical execution replay, fill models, latency/slippage assumptions. Live broker account wiring. Yes. Mandatory for every strategy path.
Dashboard Read models, scorecards, alerts, visual analysis. Trade placement and state mutation. Yes. Shared operator and research analytics surface.
Most important isolation rule: strategies do not place orders. They emit intents such as OpenPosition, AdjustPosition, ExitPosition, or TargetExposure. The system decides whether those intents become real broker orders.

Reusable units worth isolating immediately

  • Preferred holding period and instrument universe
  • Feature subsets, model weights, and regime conditions

Strategy Intelligence Layer

The future “super intelligence” belongs in its own layer, not inside the execution engine and not hard-coded inside every strategy. The correct design is a policy and model layer that can score regimes, rank strategy variants, recommend parameters, and suggest repairs or exits while still obeying the platform contracts.

What the intelligence layer is allowed to do

  • Classify market regimes and volatility states
  • Score opportunities inside a strategy's allowed universe
  • Tune parameters inside approved limits
  • Rank candidate structures, exits, and hedges
  • Write post-trade analysis and attribution summaries

What it must never be allowed to do

  • Send broker orders directly
  • Change risk limits without human or policy approval
  • Rewrite ledger history or canonical fills
  • Promote itself straight to larger live capital
  • Bypass replay, paper, or canary stages

Strategy Families and Rollout Order

1. OptionsTrading

This should be the first serious production family because liquidity, data quality, and mechanical execution are strongest here. Start with defined-risk index and ETF strategies, event-aware premium selling, spread structures, and later volatility or dispersion variants.

Priority status: live-candidate after replay and paper validation.

2. CryptoTrading

This should be the second family. Major crypto pairs and exchange-listed crypto derivatives support continuous, rules-driven operation and clean venue APIs. Focus on liquid pairs, regime-aware trend/reversion, basis, or inventory-neutral structures.

Priority status: live-candidate after 24x7 risk controls are proven.

3. PennyTrading

This should be a controlled experimental family, not the first live capital target. It needs extra modules for fraud defense, news/catalyst verification, low-float and halt handling, dilution screening, and abnormal spread/liquidity filters.

Priority status: internal-paper and broker-paper first, live only after a large safety sample.

Data, State, and Replay

Canonical state stores

  • PostgreSQL for transactional state, ledger, configs, and audit metadata.
  • Parquet in object storage for historical market data, features, and replay inputs.
  • Redis for caches, locks, and short-lived coordination.
  • Analytics warehouse later for high-volume read models if Postgres becomes a bottleneck.

Simulation is not optional

Broker paper accounts are useful, but they are not the truth layer. TradeStation says its SIM environment uses instant fills. tastytrade resets its sandbox every 24 hours. Tradier sandbox uses delayed data. IBKR paper is valuable but still a simulation. The platform must therefore own an internal simulator with per-asset fill models, fees, slippage, latency, borrow, assignment, and market-hours logic.

Build Phases

Phase 0 Lock the contracts: manifests, intents, events, ledger schema, promotion stages, broker capabilities.
Phase 1 Build the platform kernel: market data normalization, runtime, ledger, risk, execution, internal paper broker, and dashboard read models.
Phase 2 Ship the first serious strategy family: liquid index and ETF options.
Phase 3 Add crypto with 24x7 clocks, venue failover, weekend risk, and continuous monitoring.
Phase 4 Add penny strategy support only after fraud, liquidity, and halt subsystems are ready.
Phase 5 Scale operationally: split heavy services, add analytics warehouse, expand brokers, and introduce model-serving or ML policy layers where they truly add value. Only then define real-money promotion rules.

Alternatives Considered and Rejected

Option Why it sounds attractive Why I am rejecting it
One broker everywhere Simpler on paper. Breaks as soon as you need better options support, broader markets, or crypto-specific behavior.
Microservices from day one Feels “enterprise.” Creates expensive coordination before the contracts are stable.
Strategies write broker orders directly Fastest path to a first trade. Destroys isolation, replay accuracy, and safe promotion.
Use broker paper accounts as the truth layer Seems cheaper than building simulation. Broker paper environments behave differently and hide execution-model assumptions.
One giant AI “super strategy” first Feels advanced. Without a solid platform, audit trail, and replay loop, AI just makes the failure harder to understand.

Definition of Success

System success

  • Every decision and state transition is auditable.
  • Every strategy runs through the same promotion funnel.
  • Broker outages do not corrupt canonical portfolio state.
  • New strategies can be plugged in without changing the core platform.

Strategy success

  • Strategies are compared on identical metrics and identical capital rules.
  • Live drift against replay and paper is visible fast.
  • Capital scaling is earned through stable evidence, not hope.
  • Poor strategies can be paused or removed without destabilizing the system.
